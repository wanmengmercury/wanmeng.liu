Sepsis Billing Fraud Detection

Proving what "incomplete" data can reveal — $1.8M recovered

$1.8M recovered
~30% flagged for review
Novel methodology

The Problem

Sepsis upcoding is a known problem in healthcare fraud: hospitals billing infectious disease admissions under higher-reimbursement sepsis DRGs. But claims data lacks the clinical granularity to prove it. No chart notes. Bundled procedure codes. Revenue codes that obscure what actually happened bedside. The question wasn't whether fraud existed. It was whether anyone could know from this data at all.

The Insight

Everyone framed this as an individual attribution problem: prove this provider upcoded this patient. That requires clinical chart data we didn't have. I reframed it as a statistical detection problem: prove that patterns exist at scale inconsistent with genuine sepsis treatment and then give insurers enough confidence to pull charts themselves. The incomplete data couldn't tell us who. It could tell us where to look.

Architecture

The hardest part wasn't the ML. It was defining what "sepsis evidence" looks like in data that actively obscures it.

I started by designing a clinical study protocol in collaboration with a senior physician: target DRG codes, inclusion/exclusion criteria, comparison conditions. Then I ran exploratory analysis, sepsis-to-comparison-condition ratios by year and facility, to understand baseline patterns and select a representative hospital for end-to-end pipeline development.

The critical engineering challenge was feature construction. Claims procedure codes for these admissions were overwhelmingly evaluation-and-management codes. They told you a patient was seen, not what clinical service was provided. IV fluids didn't appear in procedure codes. Antibiotics didn't appear reliably. Lab tests appeared on different days than when they were performed, because services get bundled across claims. For each of the five clinical markers (the traits that distinguish genuine sepsis treatment), I had to determine: where in the data does this evidence actually live? If not in procedure codes, can revenue codes infer it? If timing is unreliable, how loose should the detection window be?

I calibrated this explicitly, running population counts under strict-to-loose rule variants to find the threshold where clinical validity held without excluding patients whose evidence was simply recorded differently. This sensitivity analysis was the difference between a pipeline that finds nothing and one that finds the right signal.

From there: structured event tables (patient x day x clinical events), vectorized into numerical tensors for clustering. K-means over three clusters, chosen after evaluating clustering algorithms against the data's structure. The final step was an LLM agent that translated cluster centroids back into clinical narratives. Which clusters showed treatment patterns consistent with genuine sepsis, and which showed insufficient clinical evidence in the claims. Statistical clusters don't move clinical stakeholders. Narratives do.

Impact

  • ~30% of admissions flagged as "unlikely sepsis"
  • $1.8 million recovered after independent audit validated findings through manual chart review of 100 hospitals
  • Methodology proved group-level pattern detection works on data everyone assumed was insufficient
  • Sole technical contributor across the full pipeline
